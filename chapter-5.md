# What Are Maatregalu?

A **Maatre (ಮಾತ್ರೆ)** is a **vowel sign**.

Earlier, you learned the independent vowels:

```text
ಅ ಆ ಇ ಈ ಉ ಊ ಋ ಎ ಏ ಐ ಒ ಓ ಔ
```

These vowels can stand on their own.

For example:

- ಅಮ್ಮ
- ಆನೆ
- ಇಲಿ

However, when a vowel follows a consonant, Kannada usually **does not write the full vowel again**.

Instead, it attaches a **vowel sign** to the consonant.

This vowel sign is called a **Maatre (ಮಾತ್ರೆ)**.

---

# The Inherent Vowel

Every Kannada consonant already contains the vowel **ಅ (a)**.

For example:

| Letter | Read As |
|---------|----------|
| ಕ | ka |
| ಗ | ga |
| ತ | ta |
| ಮ | ma |

Notice that there is **no separate symbol** for the vowel **ಅ**.

This is called the **inherent vowel**.

Because the vowel **ಅ** is already included, it **does not have a vowel sign**.

This is one of the most important ideas in the Kannada writing system.

# Where Are Vowel Signs Written?

One interesting feature of Kannada is that vowel signs may appear in different positions around a consonant.

Some are written:

- After the consonant
- Before the consonant
- Below the consonant
- Before and after the consonant

Examples:

| Position | Example |
|----------|----------|
| No sign | ಕ |
| After | ಕಾ |
| Before | ಕೆ |
| Below | ಕು |
| Before and After | ಕೋ |

Although the signs appear in different positions, you always read the **consonant first**, followed by the vowel.

# Why Doesn't Kannada Write Two Letters?

English writes:

```text
k + i
```

Kannada writes:

```text
ಕಿ
```

Instead of writing two separate letters, Kannada combines the consonant and the vowel into a single written unit called an **Akshara (ಅಕ್ಷರ)**.

This is one of the defining features of the Kannada script.

---

# Common Mistakes

## Looking for a Separate Vowel

Beginners often expect two separate letters.

Remember:

```text
ಕಿ
```

is **one Akshara**, not two separate letters.

---

## Forgetting that ಕ Already Means "ka"

Remember:

```text
ಕ
```

already contains the vowel **ಅ**.

It is **ka**, not **k**.

# Did You Know?

Every Kannada consonant can combine with almost every vowel sign.

That means a single consonant can produce many different syllables.

Fortunately, the pattern is always the same, making Kannada much easier than it first appears.

---

# Teacher's Note

Many learners find vowel signs confusing because they appear before, after, or below the consonant.

Don't try to memorize their positions individually.

Instead, think of each Akshara as a single visual unit.

With regular reading practice, you'll begin recognizing them automatically.
